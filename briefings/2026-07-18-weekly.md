## 🌍 International Relations / Conflict

### The US-Iran War Has Expanded to Regional Infrastructure, With No Exit Strategy in Sight

**What happened:** US strikes on Iran have now entered their seventh consecutive night, hitting bridges, railways, and port facilities. Iran has retaliated against US allies — Kuwait reports its power and water infrastructure was struck, and Iran separately struck Saudi Arabia for the first time in months. The Strait of Hormuz remains functionally blockaded; tanker traffic is at its lowest level in over a month, diesel prices in the US have hit $5 a gallon (up 33% since the war began), and shipping across the Persian Gulf is near a halt. Trump notified Congress of the renewed war, triggering a 60-day War Powers clock, and Senate Democrats blocked the $1.15 trillion defense authorization bill in protest.

**Why this happened (background):** The war re-ignited after a ceasefire collapsed over the core unresolved issue: who controls the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of the world's traded oil passes. Trump had previously announced a temporary ceasefire, but declared it "over" when Iranian forces refused to yield control of the waterway. The structural problem is that the US can degrade Iranian military capacity with airstrikes but cannot force Iranian acquiescence on Hormuz without either a ground presence or a negotiated deal — and Iran has domestic political incentives to resist yielding sovereignty over adjacent waters. Iran's retaliatory strikes on Gulf allies (Kuwait, Saudi Arabia) reflect its deterrence logic: threaten US regional partners to raise the cost of continued US military operations.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*, the question is whether sustained air campaigns produce coercive compliance or entrenchment — the historical record from Iraq, Afghanistan, and Libya is deeply mixed. *Normatively*, the clash is between the US framing this as a freedom-of-navigation operation (a public-goods argument) and Iran's framing it as an act of sovereignty violation and aggression against its coast. The downstream economic clash — who bears the cost of the fuel crunch — distributes harm globally, not just to belligerents.

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the US should accept Iranian control of the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for a verified nuclear deal* — tests whether a negotiated loss of leverage is better than indefinite military escalation
- *THW invoke the UN Security Council to impose mandatory mediation on the US-Iran conflict* — tests whether multilateral intervention can override great-power vetoes in practice
- *THBT Iran's strikes on Gulf state infrastructure are justified acts of deterrence* — perspective flip: argues from the strategic logic of the weaker party
- *THBT the US Congress should invoke the War Powers Act to end unilateral presidential war-making* — tests separation of powers in live conflict

**Underlying principle in play:** **Coercive diplomacy vs. escalation dominance.** The pro-coercion case: credible military pressure is what eventually brings adversaries to the table — Hiroshima, the Libya nuclear deal, even the 2003 Iraq moment. Without sustained pain, Iran has no incentive to concede Hormuz. *The rebuttal:* coercion works when the coerced party has an off-ramp that preserves regime survival; when the demand is territorial-adjacent (control of waters Iran considers its backyard), escalation dominance is a fantasy — you can bomb a country indefinitely and still not change its strategic calculus, as Afghanistan demonstrated for 20 years. What tips this case toward the skeptical view is that Iran's retaliatory strikes on Saudi Arabia and Kuwait show it is *widening* rather than de-escalating — the classic sign that coercion is backfiring into entrenchment. The same tension recurs in Russia-Ukraine (bombing Kyiv hasn't broken Ukrainian will) and in the Israeli-Hamas conflict (air dominance hasn't translated into strategic resolution).

## 🌍 International Relations / Conflict

### Ukraine's Defence Minister Sacked Amid Military Civil-Military Crisis — Soldiers and Public Push Back

**What happened:** President Zelensky dismissed Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — the young, tech-forward minister who built Ukraine's drone warfare program — triggering street protests and open dissent from soldiers at the front. Fedorov, speaking from an underground car park in a widely circulated video, delivered the most dramatic public critique of Ukraine's military command to emerge during the war. Zelensky is now reportedly also considering sacking the commander-in-chief. The German army's land forces chief separately said this week that learning from Ukraine's battlefield experience is essential to rebuilding the Bundeswehr as US support wanes.

**Why this happened (background):** The firing reflects a deep structural tension between Ukraine's two military cultures: the older, conventional brass who favour artillery and fortified positions, and the younger generation of tech innovators (Fedorov's cohort) who built Ukraine's drone advantage and see the drone as the decisive weapon of this war. The conventional command resented civilian interference in procurement and operational doctrine; Fedorov's drone-first budget priorities apparently clashed directly with the General Staff's requests. Zelensky, facing a grinding war of attrition with no breakthrough in sight, appears to have sided with the institution over the innovator — a politically costly choice given Fedorov's popularity.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*: does drone-centric warfare require a civilian innovation culture that bypasses traditional military hierarchy, or does operational effectiveness require unified command even if it slows procurement innovation? *Normatively*: in a democracy at war, should civilian ministers have real authority over military doctrine, or does wartime require subordinating civilian innovation to military expertise?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT civilian defence ministers should have final authority over military procurement doctrine even in active wartime* — tests democratic control vs. operational expertise
- *THW prioritise drone innovation over conventional military hierarchy in NATO doctrine* — tests whether Ukraine's model should be generalised
- *THBT Zelensky's sacking of Fedorov was a strategic mistake* — tests the value of institutional loyalty vs. innovation culture
- *THW allow soldiers to publicly criticise strategic decisions by their civilian commanders* — perspective flip on military speech rights

**Underlying principle in play:** **Civilian democratic control vs. military institutional autonomy.** The case for civilian supremacy: democratic legitimacy requires that elected governments direct military strategy, not the other way around — and Fedorov's drone program has objectively been Ukraine's most effective asymmetric capability. *The rebuttal:* civilian ministers making procurement and doctrinal decisions that cut across operational command create coordination failure at the worst possible time — the German army learned this in WWI, and Ukraine's front-line commanders reportedly blame drone procurement politics for supply shortfalls. What tips this case is that the *specific innovation being suppressed* (drones) has already proven decisive in this war — meaning the institutional pushback is not just conservative-bureaucratic but actively counterproductive to Ukrainian survival. The same tension recurs in any high-stakes organisation where bureaucratic incumbents resist disruptive capability: see NASA after Challenger, or the Pentagon's slow adoption of special operations forces.

## 💰 Finance & Economics

### The Strait of Hormuz Closure Is the First Real Test of Whether US Financial Supremacy Can Survive Its Own Foreign Policy

**What happened:** Oil prices have surged to their highest level in a month, diesel in the US hit $5 a gallon, and the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of globally traded oil flows — has seen tanker traffic near zero. Chevron is exploring a pipeline bypass route. The Economist this week ran a simultaneous piece warning that US payments firms may be the first casualties of eroding American financial supremacy, while the NYT reported that a new Russia sanctions bill — though symbolically important — risks accelerating the dollar workaround infrastructure adversaries have been building. Fuel price spikes are now feeding directly into US consumer inflation, with the Fed already reluctant to cut rates.

**Why this happened (background):** The structural vulnerability is well understood but politically unaddressed: US dollar supremacy rests on the petrodollar system, where oil is priced and settled in dollars globally. Any sustained disruption to Persian Gulf oil flows raises energy costs worldwide but also accelerates the incentive for non-US actors — China, India, Gulf states — to settle oil trades in non-dollar currencies, as they have begun doing incrementally since 2022. The Iran war creates the sharpest test yet of whether that diversification can go faster under crisis conditions. Meanwhile, the domestic political constraint is real: Trump has every incentive to project strength via strikes but every incentive to avoid a recession from his own war's energy shock.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*: how quickly can non-dollar oil settlement mechanisms scale under pressure? Does a prolonged Hormuz closure actually accelerate dollar decline or just impose temporary pain? *Normatively*: should the US treat financial supremacy as a strategic asset to be preserved by restraining its own foreign policy, or is financial supremacy itself a consequence of geopolitical dominance that should be pursued regardless?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the US should prioritise reopening the Strait of Hormuz through a negotiated settlement over maintaining maximum pressure on Iran* — tests whether economic self-interest should constrain foreign policy
- *THW impose a windfall tax on oil companies profiting from the Hormuz closure* — tests the domestic distributive politics of war economics
- *THBT the petrodollar system is already effectively over* — tests how to argue structural decline from current evidence
- *THW condition US military action on Congressional approval of the economic impact assessment* — novel institutional check

**Underlying principle in play:** **Structural power vs. instrumental use of power.** The argument for restraint: financial supremacy is a force multiplier that makes every other instrument of US power cheaper — sanctions work because dollar settlement is unavoidable, intelligence financing is easier, allies accept US leadership partly because the financial system rewards it. Undermining it for a short-term coercive win is trading a strategic asset for a tactical one. *The rebuttal:* a US that allows adversaries to block global waterways without military response signals that financial dominance cannot be defended, which is equally corrosive to credibility. You cannot maintain financial supremacy as a scared hegemon. What tips this case: the dollar's reserve status has historically survived wars, but it has *never* faced simultaneous pressure from an active regional conflict, an accelerating Chinese alternative settlement system, and domestic political dysfunction over sanctions policy — making this moment genuinely novel. The same tension recurs in trade policy (using tariffs as leverage risks destroying the trade architecture that makes US leverage possible).

## 🤖 Tech & AI Policy

### China's Open-Source AI Gambit: Xi Pitches "Global Collaboration" While Building Strategic Lock-In

**What happened:** China's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 this week, claiming it rivals or beats leading US models on benchmarks. Simultaneously, Xi Jinping pitched "open-source AI" and "global collaboration" as China's alternative to US AI dominance, explicitly targeting the developing world. The Economist ran a companion analysis warning that when China's open-source AI is "a trap" — the dependency runs through Chinese infrastructure, data pipelines, and political conditions even when the model weights are nominally free. US stocks sold off on AI competition anxiety, with the Moonshot release directly cited as a trigger.

**Why this happened (background):** The US export control regime on advanced chips has slowed but not stopped Chinese AI development — Kimi K3 was apparently trained on domestically available hardware using efficiency innovations. China's open-source framing is strategically shrewd: it allows Beijing to position itself as the democratising force in AI against US "AI imperialism," appeal to the Global South's resentment of US tech dominance, and create dependency relationships through model adoption before the lock-in question becomes visible. The Pew Research Center published this week showing global opinion shifting toward China over the US — the AI narrative is part of that story.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*: does adopting Chinese open-source AI create meaningful dependency risks, or is "open-source" meaningfully more sovereign than proprietary US models? The Economist's argument is that the dependency runs through inference infrastructure, fine-tuning data, and political leverage — not model weights alone. *Normatively*: should countries prioritise AI sovereignty (expensive, slower), US-aligned AI (cheaper but politically conditioned), or Chinese open-source (nominally free but strategically entangled)?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT developing countries should adopt Chinese open-source AI models over US proprietary alternatives* — forces the debate to argue from the Global South perspective, not the US-China bilateral frame
- *THW mandate that AI models used in critical national infrastructure be domestically developed or independently audited* — tests sovereignty vs. efficiency
- *THBT Xi's open-source AI pitch is more strategically dangerous than US export controls* — tests which form of AI dominance creates greater structural harm
- *THW ban the use of foreign AI models in government services* — tests the extreme sovereignty position

**Underlying principle in play:** **Openness as leverage vs. openness as genuine commons.** The pro-openness case: open-source AI genuinely reduces barriers to entry, allows independent auditing, and prevents any single actor from controlling an infrastructure that shapes everything from healthcare to governance. Restricting it on sovereignty grounds mirrors failed protectionist logic in every previous technology wave. *The rebuttal:* "open-source" is not a sufficient condition for sovereignty when the supply chain for compute, training data, and deployment infrastructure remains controlled by one actor — and China's record on technology relationships (Huawei, TikTok, Belt and Road digital infrastructure) consistently shows that apparent openness is a vector for dependency, not independence. What tips this case is a specific observable fact: unlike Linux or Firefox, large AI models require ongoing compute infrastructure and fine-tuning pipelines that are not themselves open, meaning the "open" label applies to only one layer of a deeply stratified dependency. The same tension recurs in telecommunications (Huawei 5G debate) and in global health (vaccine IP and COVAX dependency).

## ⚖️ Law / Courts

### Trump's DOJ Targets Big Law Again — and the ICC Chief Prosecutor Faces a Survivor's Testimony

**What happened:** The Department of Justice ordered the managing partners of top law firms — those that had previously reached capitulation agreements with the Trump administration — to sit for depositions about those agreements, signalling a second wave of pressure on Big Law. Separately, the accuser of ICC Chief Prosecutor Karim Khan broke her silence this week, giving a detailed account of alleged sexual misconduct, days before a vote at the ICC Assembly of State Parties that will determine whether Khan keeps his job.

**Why this happened (background):** The Big Law situation reflects a deliberate administration strategy: having extracted concessions (pro bono commitments, dropping adverse litigation) from major firms in round one, the DOJ now appears to be using the deposition mechanism to create further chilling effects, audit compliance, and potentially expose partners to individual liability. The law firms that capitulated did so partly to protect their government-facing practices; depositions now threaten to make that calculation publicly visible. On Khan: the ICC has been structurally vulnerable since the US, Russia, and China are non-parties, and Khan's credibility was already damaged by prior UN investigations into his conduct. The timing — survivor testimony just before a retention vote — maximises political pressure on states that have long overlooked institutional dysfunction at the court.

**The core clash:** On Big Law: *empirically*, do capitulation deals actually insulate firms from further pressure, or do they invite escalation? The DOJ's behaviour this week suggests the latter. *Normatively*: should lawyers have a duty to resist government pressure on their clients even at institutional cost, or is accommodation a legitimate professional judgment? On the ICC: *empirically*, can an institution lose its chief prosecutor mid-term and retain credibility for pending arrest warrants? *Normatively*: does the presumption of innocence apply differently to the head of an institution whose mandate is to hold others accountable?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT law firms that capitulated to Trump administration pressure bear responsibility for the subsequent erosion of judicial independence* — tests institutional complicity
- *THW remove Karim Khan from the ICC pending the outcome of misconduct investigations* — tests due process vs. institutional integrity
- *THBT the Trump administration's targeting of law firms constitutes an unconstitutional chilling effect on the right to counsel* — tests First and Sixth Amendment arguments
- *THW require law firms to disclose all government settlement agreements to their existing clients* — tests transparency obligations

**Underlying principle in play:** **Institutional integrity vs. pragmatic accommodation.** The case for accommodation: institutions (law firms, international courts) that refuse all compromise with political power get destroyed — and a destroyed institution helps no one. Negotiated survival is better than principled extinction. *The rebuttal:* institutional integrity is not separable from the institution's function — a law firm that has agreed not to litigate against the government cannot provide independent counsel; an ICC prosecutor who faces credible misconduct allegations cannot credibly prosecute heads of state. Accommodation doesn't preserve the institution; it hollows it out while keeping the name. What tips this case: the DOJ depositions this week show empirically that accommodation invited further pressure rather than buying protection — the capitulating firms are *more* exposed now than firms that refused to deal. The same tension recurs in academic freedom (universities signing compliance agreements with governments) and press freedom (news organisations that pre-clear stories to avoid access loss).

## 🌎 Environment

### EU Emissions Trading Rollback and Canada's Uncontrollable Wildfires Converge on the Same Political Failure

**What happened:** The European Union proposed slowing emissions cuts for businesses under its Emissions Trading System (ETS) this week, giving companies more time to reduce carbon output in a move framed as economic relief. Simultaneously, Canada is experiencing 838 active wildfires, with overnight temperatures now high enough that fires that once slowed after dark now burn around the clock — straining crews and making many fires in remote areas simply impossible to fight. Trump threatened new Canada tariffs over wildfire smoke entering US cities, while Canadian PM Carney noted that both countries bear equal climate responsibility.

**Why this happened (background):** The EU rollback reflects the same political dynamic that has eroded climate ambition across democracies: decarbonisation costs are immediate and concentrated (industry jobs, energy prices), while benefits are long-term and diffuse. The Green backlash in Europe has intensified as cost-of-living pressures make carbon pricing politically toxic. Canada's wildfire crisis illustrates the physical consequence of the exact delay the EU is now institutionalising: warming-driven fire seasons that exceed firefighting capacity are already here, not hypothetical. Trump's tariff threat is a particularly sharp instance of climate-as-trade-weapon logic — blaming a neighbouring country for the physical consequences of shared atmospheric failure.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*: does slowing ETS cuts meaningfully harm decarbonisation trajectories, or do flexible timelines produce better long-run compliance by reducing political backlash? The evidence from carbon pricing generally suggests that credibility of commitment matters more than any single phase schedule — delay signals to investment that the regime is reversible. *Normatively*: should climate policy tolerate short-term economic relief to preserve political coalitions, or is any backsliding morally impermissible given the evidence of real-time harm?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the EU's emissions rollback is more harmful than US withdrawal from the Paris Agreement* — tests which form of climate defection does more damage
- *THW impose a carbon border tax specifically on countries that roll back domestic emissions commitments* — tests whether trade leverage can discipline climate backsliding
- *THBT Canada's wildfire crisis makes the case for emergency climate powers that bypass normal democratic deliberation* — tests the precautionary vs. democratic legitimacy tension
- *THW hold wealthy states legally liable for climate harms experienced by lower-emitting nations* — classic climate reparations framing

**Underlying principle in play:** **Short-term political sustainability vs. long-term physical sustainability.** The case for flexibility: carbon pricing regimes that collapse due to political backlash (France's gilets jaunes, Australia's repealed carbon tax) produce worse outcomes than regimes that adjust timelines but survive — because the alternative to a modified ETS is no ETS. *The rebuttal:* the EU ETS is specifically designed around price signals and investment certainty; a regime that visibly adjusts when business lobbies hard enough trains investors to expect further adjustment, destroying the long-run credibility that makes the mechanism work at all. Canada's wildfires are the empirical rebuke to "we'll get there eventually" — the physical system is not on pause while we negotiate. What tips this case: the specific mechanism being weakened (the ETS) operates through price expectations over decades; short-term flexibility destroys the long-run signal even if the legal framework survives. The same tension recurs in fiscal rules (EU Stability Pact), central bank credibility, and international arms control verification.

## ✊ Social Movements

### The Afghan Women's Football Team in Exile Tests Whether International Sport Can Sustain Political Solidarity

**What happened:** Afghan women's team captain Farkhunda Muhtaj spoke this week about her ongoing efforts to lobby FIFA to officially recognise her squad — a team that exists entirely in exile, with players scattered across host countries after the Taliban banned women's sport following their 2021 takeover. The World Cup host context (the US, with the world's eyes on football) gave the story unusual visibility. Separately, protests erupted in Ukraine over Fedorov's sacking, and India saw police forcibly remove hunger-striker Sonam Wangchuk, who had been fasting for 20 days demanding the education minister's resignation.

**Why this happened (background):** The Afghan women's team case crystallises a specific institutional problem: international sports bodies like FIFA have sovereignty-respecting norms that treat member associations as legitimate regardless of the political conditions under which they operate. When a member government bans women's sport, FIFA faces a genuine dilemma — recognising an exile team implicitly contests the Taliban's legitimacy as a governing authority, which FIFA's apolitical posture resists. Yet not recognising the exile team implicitly validates the ban. The Ukrainian protests reflect a society that has developed genuine civic muscle through wartime — soldiers and civilians now feel able to publicly criticise a wartime president in ways that would have been unthinkable in 2022. Wangchuk's case shows the global hunger-strike tradition as a last resort for those shut out of institutional channels.

**The core clash:** *Empirically*: can FIFA recognition of an exile team produce any material protection for Afghan women athletes, or is it purely symbolic? *Normatively*: should international sports bodies have political neutrality as a core principle (to preserve universality), or should sport explicitly align with human rights norms even when that produces contested membership decisions?

**Motion angles:**
- *THW recognise exile sports teams from countries where participation is banned on political or gender grounds* — tests the boundary of sporting neutrality
- *THBT FIFA's refusal to recognise the Afghan women's team makes it complicit in the Taliban's gender apartheid* — strongest rights-based claim
- *THW condition international sporting event hosting on host countries' compliance with gender equality in sport* — tests leverage via hosting rights
- *THBT hunger strikes are a legitimate form of political protest in democratic states* — broader principle using Wangchuk as the anchor

**Underlying principle in play:** **Institutional neutrality vs. complicity in rights violations.** The case for neutrality: sports bodies derive their authority from universal membership — the moment FIFA starts making political determinations about which governments are legitimate, it becomes a tool of geopolitical pressure rather than an autonomous sporting institution, and powerful states will capture that tool. *The rebuttal:* neutrality is not a morally neutral position — when you apply the same rules to a government that bans women's sport as to one that doesn't, you are actively enabling the ban through the structure of your recognition. FIFA already makes political choices constantly (Russia's suspension, Israel debate); the neutrality claim is descriptively false and normatively evasive. What tips this case: the Taliban ban is not a political preference about competitive structure — it is a wholesale prohibition on women's physical activity in public. The analogy to apartheid-era South Africa (which FIFA did eventually exclude) is not strained. The same tension recurs in UN membership norms, Olympic eligibility rules, and international arms embargo enforcement.
