## 🌍 International Relations / Conflict

### The U.S.-Iran War Has Entered a Dangerous New Phase—and Nobody Has a Plan C

**What happened:** U.S. forces struck more than 170 Iranian targets this week—including air defense systems, drone and missile storage sites, port cities, and military speedboats—after Iran attacked ships in the Strait of Hormuz and fired missiles at U.S. bases in Qatar, Bahrain, and Kuwait. The Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was killed in earlier strikes; his son and presumed successor Mojtaba Khamenei has not appeared publicly, leaving a visible power vacuum. A fragile cease-fire briefly held before collapsing. Qatari and Omani mediators are trying to pull both sides back from full-scale war, with Iran's top diplomat traveling to Muscat for talks even as Ayatollah-level vows of revenge were issued.

**Why this happened (background):** The original U.S.-Israeli strikes that killed Khamenei removed the one figure who—whatever his ideology—held together the Islamic Republic's factional architecture. Without him, hard-liners face no internal check against escalation, and the attacks on shipping reflect their attempt to demonstrate relevance and extract leverage. Iran's degraded air defenses mean it cannot match U.S. firepower directly, so economic coercion through the Strait—through which roughly 20% of global oil passes—is its most potent remaining tool. The Trump administration's logic appears to be maximum-pressure through bombing until Iran capitulates, but that approach failed to produce a durable nuclear deal earlier, and bombing has now killed the interlocutor most likely to negotiate one.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Does sustained military and economic pressure produce Iranian capitulation, or does it harden regime remnants and empower hard-liners who thrive on siege narratives? This week's evidence cuts both ways—shipping traffic through Hormuz halved, suggesting Iranian leverage works; but Iran's military was hit disproportionately hard, suggesting the pressure is real. *Values*: Even if bombing "works" in degrading Iranian capabilities, does the U.S. have the right to conduct offensive strikes without a formal declaration of war or UN authorization, particularly when civilian infrastructure (steel plants, ports, railways) is targeted?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the U.S. should accept Iranian control of the Strait of Hormuz as the price of a negotiated settlement* — tests whether conceding leverage points is rational or capitulation
- *THW condition all further U.S. strikes on explicit Congressional authorization* — tests separation of powers during undeclared war
- *THBT Iran's hard-liners benefit from U.S. escalation more than they are harmed by it* — forces the government bench to defend the counterintuitive proposition that bombing empowers the target
- *TH, as Oman or Qatar, would refuse to mediate unless both parties accept a binding ceasefire first* — tests the logic of conditional third-party diplomacy

**Underlying principle in play:** **Coercive pressure vs. negotiated settlement in adversarial states.** The case *for* maximum pressure: Iran only negotiated the 2015 JCPOA when sanctions were biting hardest; regime actors respond to cost imposition, not goodwill. The *response*: the JCPOA collapsed under the same logic applied by Trump in 2018, and coercion without a credible off-ramp produces martyrdom politics. The specific fact that tips this case is the *leadership vacuum*—there is currently no Iranian decision-maker with both the authority and the political survivability to sign an agreement that looks like surrender. Coercion works when there is someone on the other side capable of accepting terms; right now there may not be. The same tension recurs in North Korea policy and in Russia-Ukraine ceasefire negotiations.

## 💰 Finance & Economics

### The Strait of Hormuz Shock Is Fracturing Global Energy Markets in Real Time

**What happened:** Shipping data showed the number of vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz halved within days of renewed Iranian attacks on commercial ships. Oil prices jumped sharply, then wobbled as markets tried to price an ambiguous ceasefire. The IMF revised global output growth down to 3% for 2026, citing high commodity prices partly driven by the conflict. At the same time, the Bank of England's chief economist signaled that inflationary pressure may require a rate *rise* this year—a remarkable reversal given the recent cutting cycle.

**Why this happened (background):** The Strait is the world's most important oil chokepoint: roughly 20-21 million barrels per day flow through it. Insurance premiums for ships using the route have surged, and several major oil and LNG companies have already rerouted around Africa, adding weeks to delivery times and costs. European gas markets are especially exposed because LNG from the Gulf was plugging the gap left by reduced Russian pipeline flows since 2022. The IMF's downgrade reflects not just the direct supply shock but the cascading uncertainty premium businesses and consumers are pricing in—what The Atlantic aptly called a "war-not-war" that is simultaneously happening and not happening.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Are current price spikes a temporary risk premium that will reverse once a durable ceasefire holds, or are they revealing a structural vulnerability in the global energy system that has been papered over since 2022? *Values*: Who bears moral and financial responsibility for the economic harm to non-belligerent states—developing countries whose import bills have spiked—when the U.S. conducts offensive military operations that predictably disrupt global supply chains?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the global energy system's dependence on the Strait of Hormuz constitutes an unacceptable systemic risk that justifies strategic petroleum reserve releases as a default response* — tests collective economic self-insurance
- *THW hold the U.S. financially liable to third-party states for economic damage caused by military operations that disrupt international shipping lanes* — novel angle on war externalities
- *THBT the Iran-Hormuz crisis proves that the green energy transition should be accelerated on national-security grounds, not just environmental ones* — reframes climate vs. fossil fuel debate

**Underlying principle in play:** **Economic interdependence as deterrent vs. economic interdependence as vulnerability.** The liberal case: interconnected economies won't go to war because the costs are mutual. The *response*: Iran has *used* its ability to hurt interconnected global trade as its primary lever precisely because it is the weaker military power—interdependence is a weapon for the revisionist state, not a restraint on it. The specific fact here: Iran's attacks on ships are strategically rational even when militarily suicidal, because they impose costs on *everyone*, forcing third parties (Europe, Asia) to pressure the U.S. to stand down. The same logic recurs in Taiwan Strait scenarios and in Russia's weaponization of gas supplies in 2021-22.

## 🤖 Tech & AI Policy

### Apple Sues OpenAI for Trade Secret Theft—and the AI Hardware Race Just Got Legally Dangerous

**What happened:** Apple filed a lawsuit against OpenAI alleging that former Apple employees carried confidential information to the company to advance its nascent hardware business. Apple described OpenAI's device efforts as "rotten to its core." Separately, OpenAI's second-highest executive Fidji Simo announced she would not return from medical leave, creating a leadership gap as the company prepares for its IPO. Meanwhile, the White House has been quietly pressuring Apple and Nvidia to work with Intel as part of a government-backed effort to stabilize the struggling chip maker.

**Why this happened (background):** The AI hardware race has escalated from software competition to a battle over physical compute and consumer devices. OpenAI's push into hardware—reportedly accelerated by its acquisition of talent from Apple, Samsung, and other device makers—threatens Apple's core product moat. Apple's lawsuit is unusual because it signals a willingness to treat AI rivals as existential IP threats rather than partners (Apple integrated OpenAI's technology into Siri as recently as 2024). The government's intervention on Intel's behalf reflects a recognition that U.S. chip manufacturing capacity is a strategic asset—and that market forces alone will not save a company critical to domestic semiconductor supply chains.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Does litigation accelerate or chill the talent mobility that has historically driven Silicon Valley's innovation ecosystem? *Values*: Trade secret law was designed for industrial-era secrets; does it translate coherently to AI, where the "secret" may be knowledge embedded in an employee's cognition rather than a document they copied?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT trade secret law should not apply to general AI expertise gained at a previous employer* — forces a debate on knowledge vs. property
- *THW prohibit government-directed industrial policy that picks corporate winners in the AI hardware market* — tests the Intel intervention's legitimacy
- *THBT OpenAI's IPO should be conditioned on independent governance review of its trade secret practices* — tests whether capital markets should enforce IP norms

**Underlying principle in play:** **Innovation-via-openness vs. innovation-via-protection.** Open-circulation of talent argument: the reason Silicon Valley outcompeted every other tech cluster is precisely because California's non-compete ban allowed engineers to move freely and recombine knowledge. The *response*: trade secret protection is distinct from non-competes—it covers genuinely confidential information, not general expertise, and without it, first-movers will underinvest in R&D they cannot protect. What tips this case is the *AI-specific asymmetry*: the line between "general expertise" and "trade secret" is systematically harder to draw in AI than in traditional engineering, because model architectures and training insights exist partly in human memory. The same tension recurs in biotech (researchers moving between pharma companies) and in academic-to-industry AI transfers.

## ⚖️ Law / Courts

### The EU Orders Meta to Redesign Its Core Products—and Sets a Precedent for Platform Architecture Regulation

**What happened:** EU regulators formally ruled that Meta's "addictive design" features on Facebook and Instagram—including infinite scroll and algorithmically amplified content loops—violate the Digital Services Act (DSA). The ruling opens the door to fines and requires Meta to alter the fundamental architecture of its products for European users. Separately, Meta pulled a new AI image-generation feature from Instagram within days of launch after widespread backlash over privacy, and has faced a parallel threat from the EU over addiction-adjacent features in the same week.

**Why this happened (background):** The DSA, which came into full force in 2024, represents the EU's attempt to move from content moderation mandates to *design* mandates—regulating not just what platforms carry but how they are built. The "addictive design" finding is significant because it operationalizes a principle that was previously theoretical: that engagement-maximizing architecture is itself a harm, not just a neutral delivery mechanism. Meta's rapid retreat on the AI image feature shows how seriously it is taking EU enforcement credibility post-DSA, which contrasts sharply with its previous decade of regulatory defiance.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Can regulators meaningfully redesign platform architecture without destroying the product's commercial viability—i.e., is "non-addictive social media" a coherent concept, or will users simply migrate to unregulated alternatives? *Values*: Is algorithmic design that exploits behavioral psychology a form of harm the state has standing to prohibit, or is it protected commercial speech and individual consumer choice?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the EU's DSA "addictive design" ruling is a greater threat to free expression than to public health* — unusual side, forces opposition to defend design freedom
- *THW ban infinite scroll and autoplay features on all platforms accessible to minors* — narrows the question to the most defensible application
- *THBT platform architecture regulation should be set by democratically elected legislatures, not by expert regulatory bodies* — tests legitimacy of technocratic lawmaking
- *THBT the EU's aggressive tech regulation is causing more harm than good by driving innovation outside Europe* — classic innovation-vs-precaution framing at the regulatory level

**Underlying principle in play:** **Behavioral autonomy vs. paternalistic protection from designed manipulation.** The autonomy case: adults choose to use infinite scroll; no one is forced, and the revealed preference of billions of users is that they value these features. The *response*: behavioral economics has demonstrated that choice architecture can be designed to override deliberative preferences—when a company engineers a product to exploit dopaminergic reward cycles, "choice" is no longer operating in conditions of adequate rational agency. The specific fact that tips this case toward the regulatory position: unlike a single harmful product a consumer can simply not buy, social media platforms exhibit network effects that make meaningful exit prohibitively costly for most users, undermining the autonomy defense. The same tension recurs in gambling regulation, junk food advertising, and loot-box mechanics in video games.

## 🌍 International Relations / Conflict

### Trump Is Turning NATO Into a Transactional Cash Machine—and European Rearmament Is the Consequence

**What happened:** At the NATO summit in Ankara this week, Trump publicly berated allies on burden-sharing while privately adopting a different tone, according to reporting. NATO Secretary-General Mark Rutte is engaged in what one Economist piece called calculated flattery to keep the U.S. in the alliance. Trump floated selling advanced fighter jets to Turkey, raised Greenland and Ukraine energy policy unprompted, and conditioned U.S. support on financial commitments rather than strategic solidarity. In parallel, European governments—shaken by Trump's declaration that the Iran ceasefire was "over" without consulting NATO partners—accelerated discussions about autonomous defense capacity.

**Why this happened (background):** NATO was designed around the principle of collective security as a public good—Article 5 deterrence derives its credibility from being unconditional. Trump's transactional reframing converts it into a protection racket: pay up or the guarantee is unreliable. This is not merely rhetorical; Trump's renewed Iran strikes without consulting allies, and his willingness to announce a ceasefire end unilaterally, have made tangible what was previously theoretical—that the U.S. will conduct consequential military actions in Europe's near-abroad without allied input. Europe's response (described by Politico as "going it alone") reflects genuine strategic recalculation, not posturing.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Does Trump's pressure on NATO burden-sharing actually produce more European defense spending (which it demonstrably has since 2017), or does it erode the credibility of Article 5 in ways that increase the probability of the conflict it is meant to deter? *Values*: Is NATO a collective security institution premised on solidarity, or a defense alliance that should be governed by transactional cost-sharing logic?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT European NATO members should begin developing an autonomous defense framework that does not require U.S. Article 5 guarantees* — tests strategic autonomy
- *THW accept that NATO's transformation into a transactional alliance is a net positive for European defense capacity* — forces the unusual side
- *THBT Mark Rutte's strategy of flattering Trump to preserve NATO is more dangerous than the problem it solves* — tests whether institutional preservation justifies norm erosion
- *TH, as a European NATO member, would commit to defense spending above 3% GDP regardless of U.S. behavior* — tests credible commitment logic

**Underlying principle in play:** **Unconditional vs. conditional security guarantees.** The case for conditionality: free-riding is real, and the U.S. bearing disproportionate NATO costs for 70 years has created a structural dependency that weakens European strategic agency. The *response*: the entire deterrent logic of a collective security guarantee is that it must be unconditional to be believed by adversaries—a guarantee that depends on payment schedules is not a guarantee, it's a contract, and contracts can lapse. Putin's calculus on whether to probe Article 5 is shaped by whether he believes the trigger is reliable; Trump's rhetoric systematically creates doubt. The specific fact that tips toward the critics: unlike bilateral trade disputes where conditionality is appropriate, security guarantees must deter *before* an attack—their credibility is undermined by the *process* of renegotiation, not just the outcome. The same tension recurs in nuclear deterrence theory and in IMF conditionality debates.

## ✊ Social Movements

### West Bengal's Protests Over Sexual Violence Signal a Recurring Indian Crisis of Accountability

**What happened:** Days of protests erupted in West Bengal after the sexual assault and killing of an 11-year-old girl. The demonstrations—directed at both the state government and at Prime Minister Modi's BJP, which had promised to improve women's safety in the state—drew widespread public anger and turned violent in parts of the Baruipur area. The protests connect to a longer pattern of mass mobilization in India over gender-based violence, most visibly since the 2012 Delhi gang rape case.

**Why this happened (background):** West Bengal has seen recurring cycles of political violence and weak accountability for crimes against women, compounded by a competitive political environment where the ruling Trinamool Congress and the BJP have each used women's safety as an electoral weapon without delivering institutional reform. The BJP's presence in West Bengal has grown substantially since 2019, making the state a key battleground; the protest dynamics reflect not just grief but electoral positioning. Structurally, India's conviction rate for rape remains low, police complaints are often suppressed, and the fast-track courts introduced after 2012 remain under-resourced. The killing of a child, rather than an adult woman, lowered the usual barriers to cross-class protest participation.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Do mass protests over individual atrocities produce lasting institutional reform (faster courts, better policing) or do they produce short-term political gestures that dissipate without structural change? *Values*: When a state fails repeatedly to protect women and girls, at what point does the right to protest become a demand for a different kind of political accountability—including removal of governments, not just officials?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT India's fast-track courts for sexual violence cases have failed and should be replaced with a federal prosecution mechanism* — tests institutional design
- *THW make the conviction rate for rape cases a mandatory performance metric for state governments, with federal funding consequences* — tests accountability through fiscal tools
- *THBT mass protests over individual criminal cases are counterproductive because they politicize the justice system* — unusual side that forces a defense of institutional insulation
- *TH, as the Indian Supreme Court, would take suo motu cognizance of West Bengal's pattern of gender-based violence* — tests judicial activism vs. deference to states

**Underlying principle in play:** **Individual accountability vs. systemic reform in response to gender-based violence.** The systemic argument: prosecuting individual perpetrators, even with maximum sentences, does not change the structural conditions—impunity culture in police forces, under-resourced courts, social norms around reporting—that produce high rates of violence. The *response*: systemic reform without individual accountability creates a loop where perpetrators calculate they will be protected by the "system is broken" narrative; high-profile convictions serve as deterrents and signal that the state is responsive. What tips this case: India has *had* high-profile convictions (the 2012 Delhi perpetrators were executed) but the pattern of violence has not broken—which is the strongest empirical argument available to the systemic reform side. The same tension recurs in U.S. policing debates and in ICC prosecutorial strategy.

## 📜 History

### Trump's Push to Rehear Birthright Citizenship and the Supreme Court's Own Credibility Crisis

**What happened:** The Trump administration asked the Supreme Court to rehear its birthright citizenship case after the Court ruled earlier this term that the 14th Amendment's citizenship clause applies to children born in the U.S. to undocumented parents. SCOTUSblog's coverage this week also flagged emerging debates about "justice shopping" on the emergency docket, concerns about the Court's approach to federal spending, and Justice Jackson's challenge to textualism's dominance. Two justices—Kagan and Barrett—have been called to testify before Congress.

**Why this happened (background):** The birthright citizenship case is the most significant constitutional test of the 14th Amendment's scope since its post-Civil War ratification. Trump's request for rehearing is extraordinary—Supreme Court rehearings are rare—and signals that the administration views the ruling as politically and legally unsettled rather than final. The broader pattern at SCOTUS this term—an aggressive emergency docket, contested approaches to federal spending power, and an ideological bloc that has produced opinions critics call a "quiet coup"—reflects a Court whose institutional legitimacy is itself under public scrutiny. The historical parallel: the period after *Bush v. Gore* (2000) when the Court's interventions in political questions eroded its claimed neutrality in ways that took a generation to partially recover from.

**The core clash:** *Empirical*: Does the 14th Amendment's text ("subject to the jurisdiction thereof") support limiting birthright citizenship to children of lawful residents, or has 150 years of consistent interpretation settled the question? *Values*: Is the Supreme Court's legitimacy best protected by insulating it from political pressure (Congress, executive rehearing requests) or by making it more democratically accountable?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT Congress should strip the Supreme Court of jurisdiction over executive immigration enforcement* — tests court-stripping as a political tool
- *THW impose term limits on Supreme Court justices* — timely given current legitimacy debates
- *THBT the Trump administration's rehearing request, if granted, would constitute an unconstitutional pressure campaign on the judiciary* — tests separation of powers norms
- *TH welcomes the Supreme Court's increased willingness to revisit settled constitutional interpretations* — unusual side, forces a defense of legal dynamism

**Underlying principle in play:** **Judicial independence vs. democratic accountability.** The independence case: courts must be insulated from political pressure precisely when their rulings are most inconvenient for the elected branches—that insulation is the whole point. The *response*: a Court that operates without meaningful accountability becomes an unelected veto player on democratic majorities; term limits, confirmation processes, and congressional jurisdiction-stripping are legitimate constitutional checks, not attacks on the rule of law. The historical anchor: after *Dred Scott* (1857), the Court's refusal to revise a catastrophically wrong ruling made the Civil War more likely—the lesson being that judicial "finality" has its own failure modes. The same tension recurs in debates about the ICC's independence from member-state pressure and in constitutional courts across Europe.

## 🧠 This Week's Throughlines

**What moved:** The U.S.-Iran situation evolved dramatically within seven days. Early in the week, a fragile ceasefire appeared to hold, and motion angles premised on "both sides have incentives to de-escalate" were defensible. By week's end, the ceasefire had collapsed, over 170 targets were struck, and the Strait of Hormuz had been partially closed to commercial shipping. Any motion briefed Monday on "the U.S. should accept a ceasefire with Iran" would need to be updated to acknowledge that the mechanism for *who on the Iranian side accepts the terms* is now genuinely unclear, given the power vacuum following Khamenei's death. Debaters should track whether Mojtaba Khamenei surfaces publicly—his absence is the single most consequential unknown in the conflict.

**The throughline:** Three stories this week—the U.S.-Iran war, NATO's transactional drift, and the EU's Meta ruling—are all instances of the same structural problem: **the erosion of unconditional commitments as the foundation of international and institutional order.** Article 5 loses deterrent value when conditionalized; a ceasefire loses meaning when one party can unilaterally declare it over; the DSA's design mandate loses credibility if platforms can simply pull features and relaunch them. In each case, the underlying tension is between actors who benefit from predictable rules (smaller states, consumers, weaker parties) and actors with enough power to treat rules as renegotiable (the U.S., large tech platforms). Debaters who can name this structural pattern—rather than treating each story as isolated—will consistently outperform those who argue each case from scratch.

**What's still unresolved going into next week:** The Iranian succession question is the story most likely to develop consequentially and soonest—whether Mojtaba Khamenei is incapacitated, positioning, or consolidating power will determine whether there is a credible Iranian interlocutor for any negotiated settlement, which in turn determines whether the Hormuz shipping crisis becomes a chronic supply shock or resolves within weeks. Any motion on Iran, energy markets, or U.S. military authority should be tracked against that specific development first.
