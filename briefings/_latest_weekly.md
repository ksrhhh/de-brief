## ⚔️ International Security

### The US-Iran Ceasefire Is Already Breaking Down, and Both Sides Are Striking Back

**What happened:** Iran attacked a container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz, prompting the US to launch retaliatory airstrikes on Iranian targets — for the second consecutive night. Iran responded by accusing the US of violating their deal and striking targets linked to American forces. The Strait had only recently begun recovering traffic after a months-long disruption following a US-Iran war that began in February. Oil prices, which had briefly returned to pre-war levels, are now volatile again.

**Why this happened (background):** The ceasefire agreement reportedly contained deliberately vague language — requiring Iran to "make arrangements" for shipping passage rather than guaranteeing free transit. Iran has interpreted this to mean it can designate which routes ships take, giving it ongoing leverage over global energy flows. The Strait of Hormuz is the choke point for roughly 20% of global oil supply; Iran's ability to threaten it is one of its few asymmetric cards. Domestically, Iranian hardliners face pressure not to be seen capitulating, so continued low-level harassment of shipping is politically useful even under a nominal ceasefire. Meanwhile, the Trump administration faces its own internal split — with Vance and Rubio reportedly pursuing divergent approaches — which reduces the credibility of any US red line.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Does retaliation deter further Iranian strikes, or does it lock both sides into an escalatory tit-for-tat that ultimately collapses the ceasefire entirely? The vague treaty language created a genuine ambiguity problem: each side can credibly claim the other is in breach.
- *Values:* Should the US prioritize maintaining the ceasefire's architecture (accepting some Iranian harassment to preserve the larger peace) or insist on strict enforcement even at the cost of renewed hostilities?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the US should accept ambiguous Iranian compliance with the Hormuz deal rather than strike back* — tests whether preserving a fragile peace is worth accepting coercive Iranian behavior.
- *THW require all ceasefire agreements involving chokepoint territories to include binding third-party enforcement mechanisms* — tests international institutional design.
- *THBT Iran's continued shipping harassment is rational statecraft that the US should negotiate around, not punish* — forces debaters to argue from Iran's strategic position.
- *THW condition further sanctions relief on Iranian compliance with unambiguous transit guarantees* — tests leverage vs. good-faith diplomacy.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Deterrence vs. escalation spiral.** The deterrence argument: failing to respond to the ship attack signals that ceasefire violations have no cost, incentivizing Iran to push further. This is textbook Schelling — credibility requires demonstrated willingness to impose costs. The escalation response: retaliatory strikes give Iranian hardliners a domestic narrative of American aggression, undermining reformists and making a durable deal politically harder to sell in Tehran. The specific fact that tips this: the treaty language was genuinely ambiguous, not unilaterally violated — which means US strikes are targeting behavior Iran can plausibly claim was permitted, making the deterrence case weaker and the escalation risk higher. *Same tension recurs in: Russia-Ukraine ceasefire negotiations over Crimean airspace; North Korea's pattern of small provocations testing alliance response thresholds.*

## 🌍 Geopolitics / Post-Colonial Orders

### Burkina Faso Severs Ties With France, Completing West Africa's Break From Its Former Metropole

**What happened:** Burkina Faso's military junta formally severed diplomatic relations with France, accusing Paris of "undermining national interests." This follows similar moves by Mali and Niger, and comes amid a broader pattern of Sahel juntas expelling French forces, closing French military bases, and pivoting toward Russia's Wagner/Africa Corps presence.

**Why this happened (background):** France has maintained a network of military bases and diplomatic influence across its former West African colonies for decades — a system critics call "Françafrique." This arrangement has faced growing popular resentment, particularly among younger Africans who associate it with continued foreign interference and the failure to defeat jihadist insurgencies despite years of French military presence (Operation Barkhane). The juntas in Mali, Niger, and now Burkina Faso have successfully weaponized anti-French sentiment to consolidate domestic legitimacy after seizing power through coups. Russia has actively courted these governments, offering Wagner/Africa Corps forces as an alternative security partner with no human-rights conditionality. The broader context is a contest between Western and Russian influence in the Sahel at a moment when jihadist violence has actually *worsened* despite French intervention — weakening Paris's core justification for its presence.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Will replacing French forces with Wagner produce better or worse security outcomes for Burkinabè civilians? The early evidence from Mali is not encouraging — Wagner has been credibly linked to massacres, and jihadist territory has not shrunk.
- *Values:* Does sovereignty require the right to choose your security partners regardless of their human-rights record? Or does the international community have standing to condition partnerships on civilian protection standards?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT France should have conditioned its military presence in the Sahel on democratic governance requirements* — tests whether security cooperation should have had political strings.
- *THW recognize the Sahel juntas' expulsion of French forces as a legitimate exercise of post-colonial sovereignty* — forces engagement with the strongest version of the anti-intervention argument.
- *THBT Western governments should impose targeted sanctions on Sahel states that contract Wagner Group for security services* — tests limits of sovereignty.
- *THW establish an African Union-led multilateral security force for the Sahel to replace both French and Russian bilateral arrangements* — tests regional solutions.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Sovereignty vs. human-rights conditionality in security partnerships.** The sovereignty argument: states have an absolute right to choose their own security arrangements; France's decade-long presence failed on its own terms, and no external power has the right to insist on a continued role. The response: sovereignty cannot be a shield for choices that foreseeably lead to mass atrocities — Wagner's documented record in CAR, Libya, and Mali makes this a human-consequences argument, not just a liberal-values one. What tips it here: the juntas are not simply *choosing* an alternative; they are actively using anti-French sentiment to suppress domestic political opposition. The security partner choice is instrumentalized for authoritarian consolidation, not purely for security improvement. *Same tension recurs in: Philippines' pivot toward/away from US basing rights; Sri Lanka's Chinese infrastructure debt arrangements.*

## 🤖 AI Policy / Tech Governance

### The US Government Is Now Pre-Approving AI Model Releases — And Industry Is Alarmed

**What happened:** The Trump administration temporarily restricted access to Anthropic's model (referred to in sources as "Mythos 5" / "Fable 5"), then partially rolled back that restriction for trusted partners after roughly two weeks. Separately, OpenAI limited access to a new model citing White House security review concerns, while explicitly stating such reviews "shouldn't become the norm." China's Alibaba was simultaneously accused by Anthropic of using fraudulent accounts to illicitly extract capabilities from Claude — and DeepSeek announced plans to double its workforce on fresh funding.

**Why this happened (background):** The US government has been moving toward treating frontier AI models as national-security-relevant assets, analogous to export-controlled dual-use technologies. The logic is that sufficiently capable models could be weaponized for cyberattacks, bioweapons development, or propaganda at scale. The Anthropic restriction appears to reflect that framework — a pre-deployment security review. But the commercial AI industry is structured around continuous rapid release cycles; even a two-week delay represents massive opportunity cost and signals to the market that government approval is now a variable in product roadmaps. The Alibaba/Anthropic allegation adds a direct China-competition dimension: if adversaries can extract US AI capabilities through fraudulent access, the question of controlled distribution becomes urgent. Meanwhile, OpenAI's IPO timeline is already slipping — government uncertainty over releases makes the commercial picture harder to predict.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Do pre-release government reviews actually reduce security risk, or do they primarily disadvantage US firms relative to Chinese competitors who face no equivalent friction while doing nothing to stop capability extraction through other means?
- *Values:* Who should have authority over when a technology is "safe enough" to release — the state (which has a monopoly on security assessment but lacks technical expertise) or the developing firm (which has expertise but a financial interest in fast deployment)?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT governments should have mandatory pre-approval authority over frontier AI model releases above a defined capability threshold* — the core governance question.
- *THW ban the export of frontier AI model weights to any non-allied state* — tests the national-security frame applied to AI.
- *THBT the US government's AI security review regime will accelerate China's AI development relative to America's* — tests the empirical case against restrictions.
- *THW require AI labs to open-source safety evaluations of new models before commercial release* — tests transparency as an alternative to state approval.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Innovation vs. precaution in dual-use technology governance.** The precaution argument: frontier AI capabilities are genuinely novel, potentially catastrophic if misused, and the state is the only actor with the mandate to weigh aggregate social risk against commercial benefit. Export control precedent (semiconductors, encryption) shows this framework can work. The innovation response: every week of delayed release is a week where Chinese labs — operating with state subsidies and no equivalent review burden — close the capability gap. The precautionary approach applied asymmetrically is not cautious; it is strategically self-defeating. What tips it here: the partial rollback and the Alibaba extraction allegations appearing simultaneously suggests the US government has neither a coherent review process nor an effective access-control regime — meaning it is getting the costs of restriction without the security benefits. *Same tension recurs in: biosecurity dual-use research of concern (DURC) review panels; nuclear technology export controls.*

## 🏛️ Law & Courts

### The Supreme Court Is Systematically Narrowing the Enforceability of Rights Without Formally Eliminating Them

**What happened:** In a cluster of end-of-term rulings, the Supreme Court sided with the Trump administration on asylum seeker border procedures, allowed the ending of Temporary Protected Status for Syrian and Haitian nationals, limited the ability of foreign nationals to bring lawsuits for violations of international law, and upheld government positions on green card holders' rights. Separately, the Court struck down a Hawaii gun restriction and ruled for the Roundup maker Bayer in a dispute over cancer warning preemption. The Atlantic characterized the pattern as an era of "meaningless rights" — where the court acknowledges rights exist but closes off judicial remedies for their violation.

**Why this happened (background):** The Court's conservative supermajority has consolidated around several doctrinal tools: narrowing standing (who can sue), expanding preemption (federal law overrides state remedies), limiting the Alien Tort Statute (foreign plaintiffs in US courts), and deferring to executive branch immigration discretion. Each individual ruling can be defended on technical grounds, but the cumulative effect is to insulate government action from judicial review in precisely the areas — immigration, national security, civil rights — where executive power is most likely to be abused. The TPS decisions are particularly significant: Temporary Protected Status covers hundreds of thousands of people who have built lives in the US over years or decades; ending it without judicial check creates a large-scale displacement that no court can now effectively review.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Does restricting judicial access to rights claims actually improve government efficiency and democratic accountability (courts shouldn't make immigration policy), or does it simply remove the only check on executive overreach that operates in real time?
- *Values:* Separation of powers — courts interpreting rights vs. elected branches making policy — vs. rule of law, which requires that rights have enforceable remedies or they are not real rights at all.

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the Supreme Court's narrowing of standing doctrine poses a greater threat to the rule of law than any single substantive rights decision* — tests the procedural/remedies angle.
- *THW establish a dedicated constitutional court with mandatory jurisdiction over executive immigration actions* — tests institutional design response.
- *THBT the Roundup preemption ruling is the correct balance between federal regulatory uniformity and state tort law* — isolates the preemption question.
- *THW allow foreign nationals harmed by US foreign policy to bring claims before the International Court of Justice as a substitute for Alien Tort Statute access* — tests the international-law alternative.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Separation of powers vs. judicial enforceability of rights.** The pro-court-restraint argument: courts are not democratically accountable; immigration and national security are textually committed to the political branches; judicial intervention in these domains substitutes judicial preference for democratic choice. This is not eliminating rights but correctly locating their enforcement. The response: this argument proves too much — if rights cannot be judicially enforced against the branch most likely to violate them, the constitutional guarantee is worthless. The specific fact that tips it: the Court is not simply declining to expand rights, it is actively narrowing remedies for rights it has previously recognized — which is categorically different from deferring to democratic majorities on new questions. *Same tension recurs in: UK's debate over restricting judicial review of Home Office decisions; India's use of non-justiciable directive principles.*

## 🌡️ Climate / Environment

### Europe's Record Heatwave Is Forcing a Political Reckoning Between Adaptation and Mitigation

**What happened:** A record-breaking heatwave struck Europe this week, with roughly 150 million people experiencing temperatures above 35°C across Germany, Denmark, Czech Republic, France, and Poland. Paris suspended outdoor sports events and banned public drinking to ease pressure on health services. The UN Secretary-General issued an emergency appeal for faster fossil fuel phase-out. Meanwhile, some European politicians responded by calling for more air conditioning rather than accelerated climate action — and Germany is reportedly reconsidering coal-powered electricity given natural gas price spikes caused by the Iran war's disruption of Gulf supply chains.

**Why this happened (background):** Europe has been structurally vulnerable to heat for architectural and cultural reasons — building stock lacks air conditioning, urban design maximizes heat retention, and social norms until recently treated heat tolerance as normal. Climate change has pushed temperatures into ranges where this vulnerability becomes lethal; the 2003 heatwave killed roughly 70,000 people and heat-related mortality has increased significantly since. The current political tension is structural: rapid mitigation requires costly near-term economic disruption, while adaptation (air conditioning, urban redesign) is cheaper in the short run but locks in fossil fuel dependence and reduces political pressure for systemic change. Germany's coal reconsideration is particularly significant — Berlin had legally committed to a coal phase-out, but the Iran war's disruption of Gulf gas supply has created an energy security argument that overrides the climate commitment.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Does widespread adoption of air conditioning in Europe meaningfully worsen global warming trajectories, or is the energy-demand increase marginal relative to the lives saved and the political stability preserved?
- *Values:* Intergenerational justice (current comfort imposes future costs on people not yet born who cannot consent) vs. immediate harm reduction (people dying in heatwaves today have claims that cannot wait for systemic solutions).

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT governments should subsidize residential air conditioning as a public health intervention rather than restrict it as a carbon concern* — tests the adaptation vs. mitigation frame directly.
- *THW impose a carbon border adjustment on states that resume coal power generation citing energy security* — tests whether security exceptions should have climate costs.
- *THBT extreme weather events strengthen rather than weaken the case for nuclear power as Europe's energy backbone* — forces an uncomfortable energy-mix argument.
- *THW prioritize urban heat-island mitigation spending (green infrastructure, cool roofs) over renewable energy subsidies in cities with high heat mortality risk* — tests resource allocation within climate policy.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Mitigation vs. adaptation as the dominant climate policy frame.** The mitigation-first argument: adaptation is the logic of defeat — it accepts that warming continues and asks only how we survive it, which locks in ever-increasing adaptation costs as temperatures continue to rise. Systemic change requires political will, which is undermined when adaptation makes the status quo livable enough. The adaptation response: this is a classic "making perfect the enemy of good" problem. People dying today cannot wait for a 30-year energy transition; moreover, in democratic systems, populations that suffer extreme heat without relief will elect politicians who prioritize their immediate comfort over distant climate goals — so adaptation may actually be *necessary* for political sustainability of mitigation. The specific fact that tips it: Germany reconsidering coal is not an adaptation choice but an energy-security choice driven by an exogenous shock (Iran war) — which shows that mitigation commitments are highly fragile under geopolitical pressure, independently of heatwave politics. *Same tension recurs in: Bangladesh's coastal adaptation spending vs. demanding rich-country emissions cuts; California's wildfire suppression vs. forest management reform.*

## 📉 Economics / Trade

### Trump's 100% Tariff Threat on European Tech Taxes Could Blow Up the US-EU Trade Deal

**What happened:** President Trump threatened to impose 100% tariffs on "numerous European countries" over their digital services taxes — levies that predominantly hit large US tech platforms like Google, Meta, and Amazon. The threat came days after the EU finalized a trade deal with the US, which Trump claimed his tariff threat would override. European officials pushed back, calling the threat a violation of the recently concluded agreement.

**Why this happened (background):** Digital services taxes (DSTs) emerged in Europe because existing international corporate tax rules, designed for physical commerce, allow US tech giants to book profits in low-tax jurisdictions (Ireland, Luxembourg) while generating revenue across high-tax European markets. France pioneered a DST; the UK, Italy, Spain, and others followed. The US has consistently treated DSTs as discriminatory against American companies and has threatened trade retaliation under every administration since Obama. The 2021 OECD global minimum tax agreement was supposed to make DSTs unnecessary, but implementation has been slow and the Trump administration withdrew US support for the OECD deal, leaving the DST conflict unresolved. The 100% tariff threat is particularly aggressive because it targets finished trade goods in retaliation for a tax policy — an unusual escalation. The timing — days after a trade deal — suggests Trump is using tariff threats as ongoing leverage tools rather than settled agreements as binding commitments.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Are European DSTs genuinely discriminatory against US firms, or are they legitimate responses to a gap in international tax law that allows regulatory arbitrage? The answer determines whether US retaliation is justified under WTO rules.
- *Values:* Should trade agreements function as binding constraints on subsequent government action, or as starting points for ongoing leverage — and which model produces better long-run cooperation?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the EU should implement its digital services taxes regardless of US tariff threats* — tests whether economic sovereignty over taxation is worth trade conflict.
- *THW establish a binding international arbitration mechanism for disputes over digital economy taxation between trade partners* — tests multilateral solutions.
- *THBT the US is right to treat European digital services taxes as protectionist trade barriers deserving retaliation* — forces engagement with the strongest American argument.
- *THW condition US tech company operations in European markets on those companies paying a minimum effective corporate tax rate in each country of operation* — tests the underlying tax policy.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Tax sovereignty vs. trade liberalization.** The sovereignty argument: states have an absolute right to determine how they tax commercial activity within their borders; the US objection is not to unfair treatment of American firms but to any tax that reduces American corporate profits, which is not a cognizable trade grievance. The liberalization response: DSTs are *facially* neutral but *designed* to target US firms specifically — no significant European digital platform exists at the relevant scale, so "digital services tax" is functionally "American company tax." Regulatory discrimination in taxation is just as trade-distorting as tariff discrimination. What tips it: the OECD global minimum tax would have resolved this by creating a multilateral framework — the US withdrawal from that agreement means the US has simultaneously blocked the solution and demands other countries not implement their own workarounds. *Same tension recurs in: Australia's news media bargaining code vs. Google/Meta; India's equalization levy on digital advertising.*

## ⚖️ Governance / Democratic Institutions

### New York's Socialist Primary Wins Expose a Democratic Party Fracture With 2028 Implications

**What happened:** Progressive and democratic-socialist candidates swept New York Democratic primaries this week, with Zohran Mamdani's campaign and affiliated candidates defeating establishment Democrats in key races. The victories are being read as a signal that the Democratic base — particularly in urban centers — is moving left on Israel/Gaza, economic policy, and the party's relationship with corporate donors. Israeli officials, per the NYT, are alarmed that US political support may be on "borrowed time."

**Why this happened (background):** The 2026 midterm environment is being shaped by the Iran war, which has made the US-Israel relationship politically costly in Democratic coalition politics in ways it was not before. Progressive candidates have capitalized on anger over US involvement in both the Iran conflict and continued support for Israel's Gaza operations. The New York results follow a pattern seen in the UK Labour Party and other center-left parties globally: prolonged time in opposition, combined with a mobilized young urban base, pulls the party toward positions that win primaries but face more uncertain general-election tests. The broader context is a Democratic Party without a clear post-Biden identity, making these primary results disproportionately influential in setting the party's 2028 direction.

**The core clash:**
- *Empirical:* Do progressive primary victories reflect durable coalition shifts that will translate to general elections, or do they reflect a primary-voter composition that systematically misrepresents the median general-election voter — setting up future losses?
- *Values:* Should political parties function as broad coalitions optimized for electability, or as ideological expressions of their most committed members — and who gets to decide which values the party represents?

**Motion angles:**
- *THBT the Democratic Party should embrace democratic-socialist primary winners rather than attempt to moderate them for general elections* — tests party identity vs. electability.
- *THW introduce open primaries in US states to prevent minority-faction capture of party nominations* — tests primary system design.
- *THBT progressive Democrats are right that unconditional support for Israel is an electoral liability for the party in 2028* — tests the foreign-policy-domestic-politics link.
- *THW establish a ranked-choice voting system for US congressional primaries nationally* — tests structural reform as a response to polarization.

**Underlying principle in play:** **Ideological authenticity vs. electoral coalition management in political parties.** The authenticity argument: parties that constantly moderate to chase median voters lose the mobilized activist base that does the ground-level work of campaigning; moreover, voters consistently say they want politicians who believe something. The coalition management response: the purpose of a political party in a first-past-the-post system is to win elections and govern — a party that wins primaries and loses general elections is not a vehicle for change but an exercise in expressive politics. The specific fact that tips it: the 2026 midterm environment is not a general election — the New York results tell us about primary electorates, not swing voters, and the FT notes that Trump's power is waning but "Trumpism" may persist, suggesting the general-election environment remains uncertain. *Same tension recurs in: UK Labour's 2017-2019 Corbyn experiment; French left's NUPES coalition fragmentation.*
